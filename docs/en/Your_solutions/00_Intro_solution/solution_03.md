# Proportionality and the Universal Law of Gravitation

## Useful Definitions and Formulas

### 1. Universal Law of Gravitation

The **Universal Law of Gravitation**, formulated by Isaac Newton, describes the gravitational force between two objects with mass.

The formula is

$$
F=G\frac{m_1m_2}{r^2}
$$

where:

- $F$ is the gravitational force  
- $G$ is the gravitational constant  
- $m_1,m_2$ are the masses of the two objects  
- $r$ is the distance between the centers of the objects  

This formula shows that:

- The force is **directly proportional** to the product of the masses.
- The force is **inversely proportional** to the square of the distance.

---

### 2. Direct Proportionality

If two quantities are directly proportional, increasing one increases the other by the same factor.

From the formula:

$$
F\propto m_1m_2
$$

This means if the masses change, the force changes by the same proportional factor.

---

### 3. Inverse Square Proportionality

The force is inversely proportional to the square of the distance:

$$
F\propto\frac{1}{r^2}
$$

This means:

- If distance doubles, the force becomes **four times smaller**.

---

### 4. Comparing Two Situations

If the parameters change, we compare the **new force** $F_{new}$ with the **original force** $F_{old}$.

Using proportionality:

$$
F\propto\frac{m_1m_2}{r^2}
$$

So the ratio of forces is

$$
\frac{F_{new}}{F_{old}}=
\frac{m'_1m'_2}{m_1m_2}\cdot\frac{r^2}{r'^2}
$$

where $m'_1,m'_2,r'$ are the new values.

---

# Step-by-Step Solution

The original gravitational force is

$$
F=G\frac{m_1m_2}{r^2}
$$

Now the conditions change:

- distance **doubles** → $r'=2r$
- mass $m_1$ **halved** → $m'_1=\frac{m_1}{2}$
- mass $m_2$ **halved** → $m'_2=\frac{m_2}{2}$

---

# Step 1: Write the New Force Formula

Substitute the new values into the gravitational formula.

$$
F_{new}=G\frac{\left(\frac{m_1}{2}\right)\left(\frac{m_2}{2}\right)}{\left(2r\right)^2}
$$

---

# Step 2: Simplify the Mass Part

Multiply the masses:

$$
\left(\frac{m_1}{2}\right)\left(\frac{m_2}{2}\right)
=\frac{m_1m_2}{4}
$$

So the numerator becomes

$$
\frac{m_1m_2}{4}
$$

---

# Step 3: Simplify the Distance Part

Square the new distance:

$$
\left(2r\right)^2=4r^2
$$

So the denominator becomes

$$
4r^2
$$

---

# Step 4: Substitute Everything Back

Now the new force becomes

$$
F_{new}=G\frac{\frac{m_1m_2}{4}}{4r^2}
$$

---

# Step 5: Simplify the Fraction

Combine the fractions:

$$
F_{new}=G\frac{m_1m_2}{16r^2}
$$

---

# Step 6: Compare With the Original Force

The original force was

$$
F=G\frac{m_1m_2}{r^2}
$$

Now compare:

$$
F_{new}=G\frac{m_1m_2}{16r^2}
$$

Therefore

$$
F_{new}=\frac{F}{16}
$$

---

# Final Result

The gravitational force becomes

$$
\frac{1}{16}
$$

of the original force.

This means the force is **16 times smaller** than before.
